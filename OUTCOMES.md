# Amplifier-Canvas Outcomes

Outcomes are measurable targets — not aspirations, not features, not tasks. Each outcome answers: "How do we know this is working?" If we can't measure it, it doesn't belong here.

These outcomes are ordered by priority. If we have to choose, we work top-down.

## Outcome 1: Daily Driver Adoption

Current CLI users on the team switch to Amplifier-Canvas as their primary way of working with Amplifier within 2 weeks of launch.

Measured by: The Amplifier team — Chris, Salil, Samuel, Alex, Ken, Manoj, Gurkaran — use Amplifier-Canvas for the majority of their Amplifier work for 5 consecutive working days, without being asked to.

Why this is #1: If nobody switches, nothing else matters. This is the gate.

## Outcome 2: Respect Attention

Amplifier-Canvas directs the user's attention to what matters, instead of requiring them to figure out what to check, when to check, and where to look.

Measured by: Users report they no longer miss completed sessions, no longer forget about long-running tasks, and no longer wonder "did something happen while I wasn't looking?" The workspace tells them what needs attention — they don't have to go find it.

Why this matters: Attention is the scarcest resource a builder has. Every minute spent hunting for status, switching apps, or wondering "is it done yet?" is a minute not spent building. If the workspace demands attention instead of directing it, it's just another app to manage.

## Outcome 3: Multi-Project Stays Organized

Users with multiple active projects can navigate between them without the workspace becoming cluttered or confusing.

Measured by: A user with 5+ projects can find and switch to any project quickly. The workspace scales with the number of projects — it doesn't degrade into a wall of tabs or windows.

Why this matters: Every Amplifier user works on multiple things. If the workspace only works cleanly for one or two projects, users will outgrow it fast.

## Outcome 4: Fast or Fail

Amplifier-Canvas is never slower than the CLI for any operation the CLI already handles.

Measured by: Opening a project, switching between projects, viewing session status — all feel instant. If a user ever thinks "this would have been faster in the terminal," that's a bug.

Why this matters: This is the adoption killer. Developers will tolerate a lot of things, but they won't tolerate slow. One laggy interaction and they're back in the terminal permanently.
